## A. Killer Hooks (Proven Scroll-Stoppers)

### 1. The Confession Open
> "I almost quit this business 6 months in."
> "I lost everything. Twice."
> "I used to be the guy making excuses."

WHY IT WORKS: Vulnerability is pattern-interrupt. Nobody expects the successful guy to lead with failure.

### 2. The Specific Number
> "$269,288. That's what I had left."
> "143 policies. That's all it takes to hit $100K."
> "I made $2,000 my first month."

WHY IT WORKS: Specificity = credibility. Rounded numbers feel fake. Exact numbers feel real.

### 3. The "You're Doing It Wrong"
> "You don't have a lead problem. You have a follow-up problem."
> "Your insurance license won't make you rich."
> "Stop buying cheap leads."

WHY IT WORKS: Challenges the reader's current behavior. Creates tension they need to resolve by reading.

### 4. The Question They Can't Ignore
> "What would you do if you couldn't fail?"
> "When's the last time you did something uncomfortable?"
> "If 92% fail, what makes you think you're different?"

WHY IT WORKS: Forces internal dialogue. The reader starts answering before they finish reading.

### 5. The Time Stamp
> "6 AM. Arizona. Christmas morning."
> "October 2024. They cut me off."
> "July 12, 2013. My last drink."

WHY IT WORKS: Drops the reader into a scene instantly. Cinematic. No setup needed.

## B. Post Structures That Convert

### Structure 1: The Micro-Story
- Hook (1 line)
- Scene (2-3 sentences, sensory details)
- Conflict (what went wrong)
- Twist (what actually happened)
- Lesson (1 sentence, "real simple")
- CTA question

EXAMPLE SKELETON:
> [Hook: Surprising claim]
> So I'm sitting there at [specific place] and [specific thing happens].
> I'm thinking [honest internal reaction].
> But then [the twist].
> [Lesson in one sentence.]
> [Question for audience]
> Never Settle.

### Structure 2: The Math Post
- Hook: surprising number
- Walk through the arithmetic step by step
- "Think about that" moment
- Reframe: "It's not as hard as you think"
- CTA: "What's your number?"

### Structure 3: The Anti-List
- Hook: "X things NOT to do"
- 3-5 things with one sentence each
- Flip: "Instead, do THIS"
- One sentence punch
- CTA question

### Structure 4: The Before/After
- Hook: contrasting two numbers or states
- "I used to..." (relatable failure)
- "Then I..." (one specific change)
- "Now..." (result with exact number)
- Lesson extraction
- CTA: "What's your before?"

### Structure 5: The Observation
- Hook: Something David noticed today
- "I was [doing mundane thing] and I realized..."
- Connect it to a bigger principle
- Analogy that makes it stick
- "Real simple" punch
- CTA question

## C. High-Converting CTA Patterns

### The Invitation (David's default)
> "What's one habit you've been putting off?"
> "Drop a number in the comments. What's your goal this month?"
> "Who else has been through this? I want to hear your story."

### The Challenge
> "Try this for 7 days. Just 7. Then tell me what happened."
> "I dare you to do the math on your own business right now."

### The Share Prompt
> "If someone you know needs to hear this, send it to them."
> "Tag someone who's been making excuses."

RULE: David NEVER uses "Click the link" or "DM me NOW" or countdown urgency. His CTAs are always invitational.

## D. Formatting Rules for Facebook

- **Line breaks matter.** One thought per line. White space = readability.
- **First line is everything.** It's what shows before "See more." Make it count.
- **Short paragraphs.** 1-3 sentences max per paragraph.
- **No walls of text.** If it looks dense, break it up.
- **Emoji: ZERO.** David does not use emojis in written content. Ever.
